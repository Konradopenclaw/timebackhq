Examples

What a TimeBackHQ recommendation could look like in practice.

The point is not AI for show. The point is a repeatable business workflow the owner can understand, approve, and keep using after the assessment.

These examples are illustrative, based on common small-business patterns and early TimeBackHQ assessment work. Real client stories will be added only with permission.

Three practical systems an owner could actually use.

Each example shows the same practical shape: what is hard today, what the assessment would recommend, what the owner sees, and where judgment stays human.

Brand awareness

Campaign kit for an owner who knows the brand but hates starting from a blank page.

An owner-led retail or gift business wants more consistent seasonal promotion, but content creation feels too open-ended. The owner still wants the voice, taste, product choices, and final judgment to feel personal.

  • Assessment finding: the bottleneck is not creativity; it is repeatability.
  • Recommended system: Claude drafts campaign angles from an approved voice guide; Canva holds reusable visual templates.
  • Owner sees: a small campaign board with draft copy, template links, image needs, approval status, and launch checklist.
  • Guardrail: the owner approves the featured products, final words, visuals, and publish decision.

Lead follow-up

Follow-up system for a service business where good opportunities quietly go stale.

A contractor, consultant, or local service owner has referrals, estimates, and past customers spread across email, text, and memory. The lost time is not one giant failure; it is the daily drag of remembering who needs what next.

  • Assessment finding: leads need a visible next-step date and simple status language.
  • Recommended system: a light CRM or spreadsheet-backed pipeline with approved follow-up templates.
  • Owner sees: a weekly list of stalled opportunities, draft follow-ups, and decisions needed.
  • Guardrail: AI can draft messages, but the owner approves tone, timing, pricing, and commitments.

Business knowledge

Reference workspace for a business that keeps reusing the same expertise the hard way.

A business has useful knowledge trapped in old proposals, estimate notes, reference books, SOPs, product notes, or the owner’s head. The work is not ready for autonomy, but it is ready for better first drafts and checklists.

  • Assessment finding: the owner needs a controlled way to reuse approved knowledge.
  • Recommended system: a Claude Project or similar reference workspace loaded with approved business material.
  • Owner sees: draft scopes, prep notes, missing-information checks, and reusable checklists.
  • Guardrail: pricing, legal language, client commitments, and final recommendations stay human-reviewed.

What a first week could look like.

The starting point should be boring in the best possible way: one useful workflow, tested against real work, with owner judgment still intact.

Day 1Confirm the stuck workflow, owner rules, and what must never be automated without approval.
Day 2Build the first template, board, checklist, or reference workspace.
Day 3Test it against real examples from the business instead of fake demo data.
Day 4Owner reviews, edits, rejects weak outputs, and locks in the first usable version.
Week 2Monitor whether it saves time, improves consistency, and deserves another small build.
